# Anti-AI Writing Style Guide

Avoid the following patterns common in LLM-generated writing.

---

## Vocabulary to avoid

These words are statistically overrepresented in AI output. Avoid them, especially in clusters:

**Significance/importance:** pivotal, crucial, vital, significant, groundbreaking, renowned, profound, enduring, lasting, indelible, testament, landmark

**Analytical filler:** highlight (as a verb), underscore, emphasize, showcase, reflect, symbolize, foster, cultivate, encompass, align with, resonate with

**Vague grandiosity:** vibrant, rich (figurative), nestled, in the heart of, boasts, tapestry (abstract), landscape (abstract), interplay, intricacies

**AI-isms:** Additionally (sentence-opener), delve, garner, enhance, valuable insights, evolving landscape, key (as adjective), serves as / stands as / marks / represents (instead of "is")

---

## Structural patterns to avoid

**Undue significance inflation** — Don't pad mundane facts with statements about their broader importance, legacy, or societal impact. Avoid: "marking a pivotal moment in...", "contributing to the broader...", "setting the stage for...", "reflecting its enduring significance."

**Challenges + Future Prospects formula** — Don't end sections with "Despite its [positive thing], [subject] faces challenges..." followed by vague optimism about future initiatives.

**Superficial analysis via -ing phrases** — Avoid attaching analytical commentary to factual statements via present participles: "...highlighting its cultural significance," "...underscoring the importance of..."

**Promotional tone** — Avoid language that reads like marketing copy, especially for neutral subjects: "breathtaking," "commitment to excellence," "active social media presence."

**Vague attribution / weasel wording** — Don't attribute claims to unnamed authorities: "experts argue," "observers have noted," "industry reports suggest," "several sources indicate."

**Rule of three overuse** — Avoid reflexively grouping things in threes for rhetorical effect: "adjective, adjective, and adjective" or "phrase, phrase, and phrase."

**False ranges** — Avoid "from X to Y" constructions where X and Y aren't actually on the same scale or don't imply a meaningful continuum.

**Negative parallelisms** — Avoid "not only X but Y," "it's not just about X, it's about Y," "not X — it's Z" constructions used to appear balanced or thoughtful.

**Elegant variation** — Don't cycle through synonyms to avoid repeating a word. Repetition is preferable to "protagonist / key player / eponymous character."

**Overcapitalized headings** — Use sentence case for section headings, not Title Case For Every Word.

**Unnecessary tables** — Don't convert prose that would read naturally into a two-column table.

**Excessive bold** — Don't bold key terms throughout body text in a "key takeaways" fashion.

**Emoji in headings or bullets** — Don't use emoji to decorate structure.

**Collaborative communication bleed** — Don't include meta-commentary like "I hope this helps," "Would you like me to expand on this?", "Here is a template you can customize," or "Let me know if you have questions."

---

## Tone principles

- Use "is/are" instead of "serves as / stands as / marks / represents" when simpler
- Prefer specific, unusual facts over generic positive descriptions
- Don't editorialize on importance unless directly relevant
- Avoid conservation-status boilerplate and ecosystem-connection filler for biology topics
- Don't claim a subject "maintains an active social media presence"
